## 0. The one-paragraph answer for the world-builder

For most of its first ~2 billion years Earth was, to first order, a
**water world with subdued relief**: continental crust existed early but sat
**mostly flooded**, emergent land was a **few percent** of the surface (not
today's ~29%), continental **freeboard was low or negative**, maximum relief
was roughly **half** the modern value (~3–5 km vs >8 km today), and — the part
that cuts against the placeholder — the **ocean basins were as deep or deeper**
than today's (~5–6 km), *not* shallow. Modern bimodal hypsometry
(continental-platform mode near sea level + abyssal mode near −4.5 km)
**assembled gradually** and only reached a modern-like configuration around
**2.5–2.2 Ga**, at the Archean–Proterozoic transition. So a mostly-drowned,
low-freeboard, low-land-fraction world is genuinely *more* faithful to early
Earth than modern bimodality — **but a shallow ocean is faithful to no era**,
and even a flooded Archean still had a crustal density contrast, so its
elevation distribution was a *compressed/partly-drowned* one, not a spike sitting
exactly at sea level. Details and the target table follow.

## 1. The "water world" question — how submerged was early Earth?

**Consensus direction: strongly submerged in the early-to-mid Archean, with
global emergence a late-Archean event (~2.5 Ga).** The disagreement is over
*how* submerged and *why* (little land vs. much water).

- **Flament et al. (2008), EPSL** — *"A case for late-Archaean continental
  emergence from thermal evolution models and hypsometry."* The anchor paper.
  Models emerged-land area as a function of mantle temperature, continental
  area, and hypsometry. Result: **emerged land was <12%, probably ~2–3% of
  Earth's surface, vs ~27% today** [A/V — number appears consistently across
  multiple secondary sources]. Mechanism cocktail: felsic crust buried under
  thick basaltic cover, continents flooded by a near-global ocean, and crust
  *too weak to support high mountains* → little land exposed to erosion despite
  early crust extraction. Global steady emergence ~end-Archean (2.5 Ga).
  *This is the single best citation for "mostly-drowned, low-freeboard,
  low-relief."*

- **Chowdhury, Cawood & Mulder (2025), Annu. Rev. Earth Planet. Sci.
  53:443–478** — *"Subaerial Emergence of Continents on Archean Earth."* The
  current review synthesis [V — full text read via fetch; author/venue confirmed].
  Narrative arc:
  - Emergence *did* occur in the early-to-mid Archean but was **transient** and
    exposed **immature/precratonized volcanic crust** for brief periods.
  - **Stable, craton-scale** emergence began **~3.2–3.0 Ga**, tied to thick,
    stable cratonic lithosphere.
  - Emergent-land area **grew from ~3.0 to 2.5 Ga** as more cratons formed
    (likely coinciding with onset of plate tectonics).
  - **Culminated ~2.5–2.2 Ga**, when land area and freeboard **resembled
    today's**. Extensive subaerial volcanism recorded in six cratons 2.4–2.2 Ga.

- **Korenaga (2021), *Life* 11:1142** — *"Was There Land on the Early Earth?"*
  [V — open full text read]. Represents the **contrarian "more water, but also
  maybe more land"** synthesis built on early-crust-formation models:
  - Mid-Hadean exposed land could have been **~20% or more** (Guo & Korenaga
    growth model), or **~40%** if the early ocean was shallow (Rosas & Korenaga)
    — i.e. *high* early land is defensible under early-crust-formation models.
  - But by late Hadean / early Archean, exposed land may have been **limited to
    isolated ocean islands** as the ocean deepened.
  - Global steady emergence again **~2.5 Ga**.
  - Explicitly flags its own estimates as **"highly speculative."**

- **Dong et al. (2021), AGU Advances** — the **mantle-water "water world"
  mechanism** [A — full text was paywalled to the fetch; from abstract + press].
  A hotter early mantle has *lower* water storage capacity per the
  temperature-dependent storage model → more water *degassed to the surface* in
  the early Archean → larger surface ocean → deeper flooding. Popularized as the
  "Archean water world" (4–3.2 Ga). This is the *water-side* explanation for
  submergence (vs Flament's *land-side* crustal explanation); they are not
  mutually exclusive.

**Bottom line (freeboard emergence):** transient/local emergence back to
~3.3–3.2 Ga (PNAS 2021, below); **stable regional emergence ~3.2–3.0 Ga**;
**modern-like global freeboard ~2.5–2.2 Ga.** The 2–3% (Flament) vs 20–40%
(Korenaga) spread is the honest uncertainty band for the early/mid Archean.

## 2. Hypsometry — was the modern bimodal distribution present early?

**Modern Earth is sharply bimodal; early Earth was not — or was a compressed,
mostly-drowned version of it.**

**Modern reference numbers** [A — standard textbook/Wikipedia/ETOPO values,
mutually corroborating]:
- Bimodal elevation modes at roughly **+100 m** (lowland continental platform)
  and **−4,700 m** (deep-sea floor). Mean land elevation **~840 m**; mean ocean
  depth **~3.7 km**.
- **Land ~29%** of surface (project measured 29.2% for modern), **ocean ~71%**.
- Continental shelf (0 to −200 m) ≈ **5.4%** of surface; **>50%** of Earth lies
  between −4 and −6 km (abyssal plains/hills).
- Total relief range ~**20 km** (Everest +8.8 km to Challenger Deep −11 km);
  "usable" continental-vs-abyssal contrast ~**5 km**.

**Why the bimodality exists** [A]: it is a **two-population** signal — light,
thick continental (felsic, ~40 km, density ~2.7) crust floats high; dense, thin
oceanic (mafic, ~7–10 km, density ~2.9–3.0) crust floats low. Isostasy of two
crust types = two modes. **Bodies without plate tectonics (Moon, Mars, Venus)
are essentially unimodal** — a single crustal population. So bimodality is a
*plate-tectonic fingerprint*.

**What early Earth's hypsometry probably looked like:**
- The **crustal-column** density contrast existed as soon as felsic continental
  crust existed (Hadean/early Archean), so a *two-population* substrate was
  present in principle [A].
- But the **elevation** bimodality *relative to sea level* was **muted**, for
  three compounding reasons [V, from the Science Advances densification paper
  + Flament]:
  1. **Continents were flooded** — the continental mode sat *below* sea level,
     so the emergent part of the curve was tiny.
  2. **Oceanic crust was thicker** in the hot Archean — **~25–35 km** vs modern
     ~10 km — so the ocean floor rode *higher* (thicker crust = more buoyant),
     *compressing* the vertical gap between the two modes.
  3. **Relief was subdued** (§3) — weak, hot crust could not build tall
     platforms or deep trenches.
- Net: early hypsometry was a **narrower, lower-amplitude, mostly-submerged**
  distribution. Calling it "unimodal" is defensible in *elevation-above-sea-level*
  terms (one drowned hump) but the underlying *crustal* distribution was already
  two-population. The modern sharp bimodality **developed** as the mantle cooled,
  oceanic crust thinned toward ~10 km (deepening basins), and cratons thickened
  and rose — a process that ran through the Archean and locked in ~2.5–2.2 Ga.

## 3. Relief & landforms — what was even possible pre-/proto-tectonically

- **Maximum Archean relief ~3–5 km, vs >8 km today** [V — Science Advances
  densification paper, PMC11389787; explicit "3 to 5 km in the Archean as
  opposed to >8 km today"]. Roughly **half** modern relief. This is the single
  most useful landform constraint for a world-builder: **no Himalayas, no
  ~11-km trenches** on early Earth.
- **Why relief was capped** [V]:
  - **Hot, weak crust** could not support large topographic loads (Korenaga:
    early crust "unable to support large topographic variations").
  - **Eclogitization / lower-crust densification**: mafic lower crust thickened
    past ~**60 km** (≈1.8–1.9 GPa) transforms to dense eclogite and *founders*,
    self-limiting how high crust can pile. A felsic crust needs to reach
    **~45 km** to rise >4 km above the seafloor under hydrous conditions, and
    even then risks rheological collapse from heat.
  - So sustained high standing topography needs **cool, thick, strong cratonic
    lithosphere** — a *late-Archean* development.
- **Pre-/proto-tectonic landform inventory** [A/M — synthesized]: dominated by
  **volcanic constructional** landforms — basaltic plateaus/oceanic-plateau-like
  edifices, greenstone-belt volcanic piles, TTG (tonalite–trondhjemite–
  granodiorite) domes, and **isolated volcanic ocean islands** (Korenaga's
  "isolated ocean islands"). The first stable *rise* of continents (PNAS 2021,
  below) was by **magmatic thickening to ~50 km silica-rich crust** in
  **non-plate-tectonic** settings — i.e. thickening by melting/underplating, not
  by continental collision. Fold-and-thrust mountain belts (collisional) are a
  *plate-tectonic* landform and should be scarce-to-absent before ~3 Ga.
- **PNAS (2021), Bindeman-adjacent / Chowdhury et al.** — *"Magmatic thickening
  of crust in non–plate tectonic settings initiated the subaerial rise of
  Earth's first continents 3.3 to 3.2 billion years ago"* [A]. First stable
  emergence ~700 Myr earlier than many models predicted; Singhbhum craton;
  ~50-km-thick silicic crust rising isostatically.

## 4. Continental-crust growth curves — the competing models

The **central unresolved debate**. Two end-member shapes, plus the modern
zircon-isotope middle ground. This governs *how much* continental material
exists to be flooded/exposed at a given age.

| Model / camp | Curve shape | Crust volume vs present | Refs |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Armstrong (1981, 1991)** — early formation + recycling | Near-present crustal volume **from the Hadean**, ~steady-state thereafter (generation ≈ destruction) | ~100% very early | [A/M] |
| **Rosas & Korenaga (2018)** — mantle Sm–Nd box model | Present-day level by **~4.2 Ga**; formation from ~4.5 Ga | ~100% by 4.2 Ga | [V, via Korenaga 2021] |
| **Guo & Korenaga (2020)** | Present-day mass by **~4.0 Ga**; formation from ~4.3 Ga | ~100% by 4.0 Ga | [V, via Korenaga 2021] |
| **Dhuime, Hawkesworth et al.** — Hf/Nd zircon | Gradual; **~65–70% of present volume by ~3 Ga**, growth-rate *slowdown* after ~3 Ga interpreted as *rising destruction*, not falling generation | 65–70% by 3 Ga | [A] |
| **Taylor & McLennan (1985)** — sediment-chemistry | Gradual growth, **major pulse at Archean–Proterozoic boundary** (~2.5 Ga); Archean upper crust more mafic | Substantially <100% until ~2.5 Ga | [A/M] |

**Reconciling note** [A]: Modern zircon work (Dhuime/Hawkesworth) suggests
**60–80% of present crustal *volume* was generated by ~3 Ga**, yet **<25% of
*today's surviving* juvenile crust is older than 3 Ga** — i.e. lots of early
crust was made *and then destroyed/reworked*. This is why "early formation"
(Armstrong/Korenaga) and "gradual net growth" (Taylor–McLennan) can both be
partly right: **generation** was front-loaded, **preservation** was back-loaded.
For a world-builder the operational takeaway is that **continental *area*
available to stand above water grew through the Archean regardless of which
generation curve is true** — because emergence is gated by crustal
*thickness/strength/freeboard*, not just total volume.

## 5. Ocean volume through time — was there more water early?

- **Direction: likely MORE surface water in the early Archean, and DEEPER
  basins — not less.** This is the sharpest correction to the placeholder.
- **Korenaga (2021)** [V]: early Hadean ocean **shallow (<1 km)** (most water
  still in the mantle) → deepened to **~6 km by end-Hadean (~4 Ga)** as the
  mantle degassed → ocean **~50% more voluminous at 2.5 Ga** than today
  (conservative min), with **mean ocean depth decreasing ~1 km per billion
  years** since. Present mantle holds ~**1 ocean mass** of water (saturation
  ~5 ocean masses).
- **Dong et al. (2021)** [A]: temperature-dependent mantle storage → hotter
  early mantle expelled more water to the surface → larger early ocean; the
  ocean has been **shrinking** (water re-ingested by subduction) as Earth cooled.
- **Freeboard-constancy** [A]: continental freeboard has been ~constant through
  the **Phanerozoic** (±~200 m sea-level swings) and *roughly* constant back to
  the **Paleoproterozoic**; it is the *Archean and earlier* where freeboard, ocean
  volume, and land fraction genuinely differ. Freeboard constancy is the tool
  used to back out ocean-volume history — but its Precambrian application is
  debated (many proxies, large uncertainties).

**World-builder consequence:** an *early* world should if anything have
**deeper, more voluminous oceans** than modern Earth, combined with **low
land**. The placeholder's oceans being **12× too shallow** is wrong for *every*
era and is the highest-priority fix — it is what forces the drowned-sponge
unimodal shape (no abyssal population can form in a shallow basin).

**Reading the table against the placeholder (land 41.5%, unimodal, oceans 12×
too shallow):**
- **Land fraction 41.5% is too high for *any* era** and much too high for early
  Earth — target well below modern 29%, into the low-single-to-mid teens.
- **Unimodal-with-sea-level-at-the-mode is *partly* right for early Earth** (a
  drowned, low-freeboard world) — but only once the ocean basins are made deep.
  The placeholder gets the right *shape* for the wrong *reason*: it's unimodal
  because the ocean is a shallow pan (no abyssal population), not because
  continents are flooded into a deep ocean.
- **The water shortage is the load-bearing error — not basin depth** *(corrected
  2026-07-10 by the primary reads; the first version of this bullet said "deepen
  to 5–6 km," which was Korenaga's minority geometry)*. The placeholder's ~0.3 km
  pan is wrong for every era, but the fix is **low land + generous water over
  ~modern-depth basins**: derive sea level from a declared inventory (Flament
  Eqs 2–4 are the published template), cap subaerial relief ~1–1.5 km, and the
  drowned compressed-bimodal emerges honestly. Emergence through the Abyssal
  phase is then **crust-side** (thickening/maturation past ~40–45 km — Cawood's
  curve: ~40 km @3.8 Ga → ~55 km @3.2 Ga; master lever = secular mantle
  cooling), not sea-level-side.

## 7. Rejected / uncertain / needs-verification

- ~~**The exact 2–3% (Flament) figure** … [A]~~ — **RESOLVED 2026-07-10 [V]**:
  page-read at 1.8–3.1% deterministic / MC medians 1.79–2.84%; the 2–3-vs-12%
  spread's driver is max continental elevation (~3.6 km anorogenic cap vs
  modern ~9 km). `pdf-notes/flament-2008.md`.
- **Korenaga's 20–40% early-land** — genuinely contested; depends on accepting
  early (Hadean) near-complete crust formation, which the Taylor–McLennan /
  sediment-chemistry camp rejects. Present it as the **high end of a bracket**,
  not a competing consensus.
- **"Water world" as literal total submergence** — the strong form (zero land,
  ever) is **not** supported; even the most submerged models keep isolated
  volcanic islands and transient emergent volcanic crust. Reject "no land at
  all"; accept "very little, mostly transient, volcanic."
- **Oceanic crust 25–35 km** [V from one paper's melting model] — plausible but
  model-dependent; some estimates are lower (~20 km). Uncertain.
- **Ocean ~50% more voluminous at 2.5 Ga / ~1 km-per-Gyr shallowing** — Korenaga's
  numbers; other groups (constant-freeboard / constant-ocean traditionalists)
  dispute that ocean volume changed much at all. This is a **live debate**, not
  settled. The direction (early oceans not-shallower, probably deeper) is the
  safer claim than the specific rate.
- **Freeboard "roughly constant since Paleoproterozoic"** — well-supported for
  Phanerozoic, weaker and proxy-dependent for Precambrian.
- **Dhuime/Hawkesworth 65–70%-by-3-Ga vs Guo–Korenaga ~100%-by-4-Ga** — these
  disagree by design (preservation vs generation); do **not** average them.
- **Not chased (out of scope):** biosphere/GOE/atmospheric-chemistry couplings,
  banded-iron formations, specific δ-isotope proxies. Flagged only where they
  date emergence (e.g. subaerial-weathering isotope signals ~3.7 Ga, Bindeman
  2018 land-rise ~2.4 Ga) — [M/A], not load-bearing for hypsometry.
